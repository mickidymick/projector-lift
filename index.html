Dad's back-porch system — finalized purchase plan & technical reference

Prepared 2026-06-07 · AV chain rebuild added 2026-06-09 · Decisions locked after on-site visit

Hide-away ceiling box drops a projector via two linear actuators while a separate wall-mounted motorized screen rolls down. Original control electronics died after ~10 years; we're rebuilding around a permanent brightness fix and modernized control hardware. Scope expanded 2026-06-09 to also rebuild the AV chain that feeds both the indoor TV and the projector — the same wall button now drives whole-house mode switching (indoor vs. outdoor) via a new networked receiver + Apple TV. Screen-material physics (context for the rejected options)

White screens diffuse evenly → 180° viewing angle (great for a roaming crowd) → but reflect ambient light too, so they wash out in daylight. Cannot reject ambient. Fixed in this build by the L730U's raw brightness instead.

ALR screens are directional → reject ambient (~65%+) → fix daytime → but have a narrow, fixed viewing cone. The quoted angle is horizontal only. Angular-reflective ALR also has a vertical sweet spot: a high projector throwing down pushes the bright zone low, so standing viewers fall out of it both horizontally and vertically.

Diffusion and ambient rejection are physically opposed. The standing-crowd usage favors white; brightness handles the daytime problem; therefore white is the right material category here.

8 · Reliability hardening

The original "button stops working / needs a reboot" symptoms are almost certainly not caused by the Arduino itself — they're caused by the surrounding hardware design. An ESP32 wired the same way will fail the same way. Address these specifically and the rebuild becomes effectively bullet-proof.

Power & EMI

Inductive kickback from the actuators and supply sag during inrush are the #1 cause of phantom MCU resets and latch-ups.

  • 1000 µF bulk capacitor across the 12 V rail near the BTS7960 — absorbs inrush, prevents rail sag when actuators start.
  • 100 nF decoupling cap on every IC's VCC pin (MCU, BTS7960 logic side, F-1001 relay input).
  • TVS diode (SMBJ15CA or similar) across the 12 V supply — clamps inductive spikes from motor switch-off.
  • Dedicated 12 V → 5 V buck for the ESP32 + BTS7960 logic (cheap LM2596 module). Feed the ESP32's 5V/VIN pin (the onboard AMS1117 drops to 3.3 V internally), and tap the same rail to both BTS7960 VCC pins. Choosing 5 V instead of feeding the 3V3 pin directly lets you keep USB plugged in for programming/serial without two sources fighting on the 3.3 V net. Don't share this regulator with anything that switches heavy current.
  • Star-ground: motor return wires run straight back to the PSU's GND terminal, not through the logic ground rail. Logic GND and motor GND join only at the PSU.
Inputs

Floating, noisy, or un-debounced inputs cause phantom button presses, ISR storms, and lock-ups.

  • Software debounce on the momentary wall switch: ESP32 internal pull-up + ESPHome's binary_sensor with delayed_on: 30ms / delayed_off: 30ms filters. Hardware RC debounce is unnecessary when the firmware handles it cleanly. Each press fires once on the rising edge of on_press; the release is ignored.
  • Shielded or twisted-pair wire for any input run over ~3 ft (wall button, BTS7960 R_IS / L_IS current-sense lines). Tie shield to logic GND at the MCU end only — never both ends.
Firmware safety net

Even with perfect hardware, a single bad line of code can wedge the firmware. The watchdog turns "needs a power cycle" into "user never noticed."

  • Enable the hardware watchdog (esp_task_wdt on ESP32) with a ~5 s timeout. Feed it from the main loop. If the firmware ever hangs, the MCU self-resets in seconds.
  • Never use blocking delay() in loop(). Drive the state machine with millis() deltas so the button is read every iteration.
  • Mark all ISR-shared variables volatile — don't let the compiler optimize away updates from interrupt context.
  • Enforce BTS7960 deadtime (~50 ms minimum between direction reversals) — a shoot-through pulse generates EMI identical to half-suppressed kickback.
  • End-of-travel threshold calibrated to the actuators: read normal running current first, set the "end-of-travel" threshold somewhere comfortably above the noise floor but below the lowest "running" reading you ever see (0.1–0.3 V is the usual band on R_IS / L_IS). Until the IS-pin pulldown mod (next card) is installed, the high end of the IS range saturates the ADC at the on-module 10 kΩ pulldown — that's fine; current-drop detection only needs the low end, which reads cleanly. Tune empirically with the real PA-04s on the bench.
  • Stall detection re-enable plan: see the next card (IS-pin pulldown + filter mod). Mod installs 4× 1 kΩ + 4× 100 nF to rescale IS-pin voltage into the ESP32 ADC window so running / stall / EOT bands become discriminable instead of all saturating at the rail. Until then the firmware uses only the current-drop EOT signal (PA-04 internal-limit fires → IS-pin voltage collapses to ~0) plus the Travel safety timeout as the failsafe.
IS-pin pulldown + filter mod (current-sensing rescale)

The BTS7960 module's on-board 10 kΩ pulldown on each IS pin is sized for the chip's max ~43 A rating. At our actual ~4–12 A operating range the IS pin saturates the ESP32's 3.3 V ADC any time the motor is drawing meaningful current — so "running", "stall", and "EOT" all look identical (≈ rail). Drop a 1 kΩ to GND in parallel with the on-module 10 kΩ and the effective pulldown becomes ~909 Ω; current scales linearly into the 0–3.3 V ADC window so the three bands become individually readable. A 100 nF cap in parallel filters the PWM commutation transients (τ ≈ 90 µs at 20 kHz PWM — passes the motor's slow load envelope, blocks the per-period kick).

  • BOM (already on-hand 2026-06-30 from BOJACK + ALLECIN kits): 4× 1 kΩ axial ¼ W carbon-film resistor (one per IS pin), 4× 100 nF / 50 V ceramic disc cap (one per IS pin).
  • Per-channel wiring — one R and one C from each IS pin to logic GND. All 4 channels (GPIO 32 / 33 / 34 / 35) get the identical treatment. The on-module 10 kΩ stays in place — we're adding in parallel, not replacing.
  • Physical placement — easiest is solder both components on the back of the main perfboard, axial leads bridging the BTS-socket IS-pin pad and the nearest GND pad. R and C can share the same two through-holes (twist their leads together at each end before soldering) since they're in parallel. Keep the leads short (under ~½") to minimize loop area picking up motor-switching radiation.
  • Expected voltages post-mod (BTS7960 kILIS ≈ 8500, Reff = 1 k || 10 k = 909 Ω): 4 A → ~0.43 V (running idle), 12 A → ~1.28 V (full-load running), 20 A → ~2.14 V (stall lower), 30 A → ~3.21 V (stall upper, near rail). EOT signal stays at ~0 V — well below any running reading.
  • Calibrate after install: bench-run each actuator unloaded, log the steady-state IS voltage during cruise → that's your noise-floor. Set eot_v at ~½ of that floor (well above 0, well below cruise). Stall threshold goes ~30 % below the lower stall bound (i.e., ~1.5 V) — measure first, threshold second, never the other way around.
  • Validation after install: re-run the temporary endstop test cycles (no change expected — the mod is on a different signal path), then a full open / close cycle and confirm L_IS-drop fires the open_lift EOT cleanly and R_IS-drop still fires close_lift cleanly. Only then re-enable stall detection in firmware.
Deployed-position endstops (also slide-overrun protection)

The PA-04s have an 8" stroke; the reused drawer slides only extend ~7.25". Without an external limit, the actuators would push the slides ¾" past their hard stops and tear them apart. Current sensing alone won't catch this in time — stamped-steel slide stops bend at lower force than the actuators draw in normal acceleration. The endstops do double duty here: they define the deployed (lowest-travel) position and protect the slides from overrun, because the chosen deployed position is set above the slide hard stops.

  • Two Prusa endstops, mounted on the main box walls, tripped by a bracket on the inner box. Each endstop wires to its own ESP32 GPIO (INPUT_PULLUP, switch-to-GND). When endstop A trips, firmware drops EN on BTS7960 #1 only → actuator A's outputs go hi-Z and the PA-04's worm gear holds position rigidly while actuator B keeps extending until its own endstop trips. Both endpoints are hard-defined (external endstops on extend, PA-04 internal limits on retract), so sync drift cannot accumulate across cycles.
  • Split the BTS7960 EN lines. Originally ganged on GPIO27. Change to GPIO27 → BTS7960 #1 EN and GPIO19 → BTS7960 #2 EN. PWM lines stay ganged (same duty to both modules); the per-module EN drop is what gives independent cutoff. Hi-Z disable on EN = no current to motor regardless of PWM duty.
  • Endstop GPIOs: GPIO23 ← endstop A, GPIO22 ← endstop B. Use NO contacts to GND; tie the Prusa board's LED+ pin to the perfboard's 3.3 V rail (tapped from ESP32's onboard AMS1117 output, i.e. the 3V3 pin), not the 5 V rail. The Prusa board can present its VCC back onto SIG via its LED/pull-up network, and pulling SIG to 5 V would exceed the ESP32 GPIO's 3.6 V max input and damage the chip. The LED runs slightly dimmer at 3.3 V but still gives a clear "tripped" indicator. Run the SIG/GND pair as a twisted pair with the wall-button input — same noise environment.
  • Mount geometry: mount each endstop body to an inside wall of the main box; mount a small bracket on the top of the inner box positioned to trip both endstop levers when the inner box reaches the chosen deployed (lowest-travel) position. That position must sit at least ~¼" above each slide's mechanical hard stop so a touch of actuator coast (negligible — PA-04's worm gear is non-back-drivable) can't drive the slide into its stop.
  • Time-based watchdog independent of endstops. Start a timer on every extend command. If both endstops haven't tripped by T+~13 s (PA-04 ~0.6"/sec × 8" + margin), fault: drop both EN lines and require a manual button press to re-arm. Catches a dead switch, broken signal wire, or wedged lever before the side without a working endstop runs to its internal 8" limit and shreds the slide.
  • Log trip-delta per cycle. Record t_B_trip − t_A_trip and publish via ESPHome. A consistent and growing delta is the early warning that one actuator is aging — fix it before racking gets ugly. Small per-cycle deltas (< ½ sec) are normal and self-correct at the endstops.
Net effect: brown-out becomes unreachable, EMI gets absorbed instead of latching the MCU, and the watchdog catches any remaining edge case. This is what "rock-solid" looks like for a motor-driven home automation build — chip choice (Mega vs ESP32) is the smallest factor.

Fan-driver perfboard (separate small board)

The IRLZ44N MOSFET, the 220 Ω + 10 kΩ gate network, and the RB085T-60 flyback diode live on a dedicated small perfboard (~5 × 7 cm). The LM2596 #2 buck is NOT on this board — it sits standalone, takes 12 V from wherever you tap it, and feeds its 6.5 V output into this board's BUCK IN plug. Three 2-pos Phoenix plugs along the top edge: BUCK IN (+6.5 V from the external buck), SIG IN (GND + GPIO 17 PWM from the main control board), FAN OUT (+6.5 V continuous + MOSFET-switched return, to both AIRPLATE fans in parallel).

How to read this: every wire is drawn explicitly — no implicit tag-net jumps. Solid lines run on the top side of the perfboard (orange = +6.5 V, purple = FAN-switched return, green = GPIO-17 PWM gate signal); dashed lines run on the back side (all GND returns and the GND bus). Where a solid wire visually crosses the dashed GND rail or another solid wire of a different color with no dot at the crossing, that's just a visual crossover — the two are on different sides of the board (or different nets) and don't connect. 12 V power board (separate small board)

Tiny low-current distribution board: PSU 12 V comes in, passes through a single 3 A fast-blow fuse, and fans out to three downstream consumers via Phoenix plugs. Neither LM2596 buck lives on this board — both are standalone modules wired off-board. The 5 V output of LM2596 #1 routes directly to the main control board's 5 V IN plug; the 6.5 V output of LM2596 #2 routes directly to the fan-driver board's BUCK IN plug. This board's only job is to take one fused 12 V feed and split it three ways: to LM2596 #1's input, to LM2596 #2's input, and to the screen's +12 V trigger leg (paired with a SCREEN ↔ RELAY plug loop that low-side-switches the screen's − return through the F-1001 contacts).

Actuator motor current does NOT pass through this board. That lives on a separate motor fuse board (Diagram #6 below). The 1000 µF bulk cap + P6KE15CA TVS also live on the motor fuse board, not here — both exist to handle BTS-side inrush + back-EMF transients and need to sit electrically close to the BTS B+ pads.

How to read this: same convention as the fan-driver board — solid lines run on the top side of the board (red = +12 V, green = the switched SCREEN↔RELAY node), dashed lines run on the back side (GND returns). The new vertical RELAY plug sits directly below SCREEN pin 1 — pin 1 (top) is the switched-node tie to SCREEN pin 1, pin 2 (bottom) drops to the GND rail. The SCREEN plug and RELAY plug split the F-1001 wiring into two clean cable runs — one to the screen, one to the relay — by doing low-side switching of the screen trigger:
  • SCREEN plug → screen trigger pair (existing run to the VMAX2): pin 1 (SW) → screen's − trigger; pin 2 (+12 V) → screen's + trigger.
  • RELAY plug → F-1001 CH1 contact-side (short hop to the off-board relay): pin 1 (SW) → CH1 NO screw; pin 2 (GND) → CH1 COM screw.

How the circuit fires: screen + sees +12 V always (SCREEN pin 2 ← +12 V rail, direct). Screen − floats until the F-1001 coil energizes — at that moment CH1 COM (GND, via RELAY pin 2) connects to CH1 NO (the SW node, via RELAY pin 1 ↔ SCREEN pin 1 board trace), which yanks screen − down to GND. +12 V across the trigger pair = screen drops. When the coil de-energizes, NO floats, screen − floats, screen retracts.

Why the swap from the original high-side design: in the original wiring the relay sat between the +12 V rail and the screen + wire, and a GND wire ran alongside it as a passthrough. That left one orphan wire (GND) wandering through the relay area with no plug terminus. Splitting into SCREEN plug (always-on pair to screen) + RELAY plug (switched pair to F-1001) gives each plug a single destination and lets the GND rail terminate cleanly on a board pad.

Caution — confirm screen trigger isolation before powering up: low-side switching only works if the screen's − trigger input is electrically isolated inside the screen (high-impedance input, optocoupler, or floating reference). Most motorized screens that mate with AVR trigger jacks are isolated, but if the screen ties its trigger − to chassis GND internally, the relay will have no effect (screen − will always be at GND through that chassis path). Quick check before final wiring: with the screen powered and disconnected from this board, measure resistance between the screen's trigger − terminal and the screen's chassis. Open (or very high) = isolated, low-side switching will work. Continuity = the screen ties − to chassis; in that case swap to a high-side design (SCREEN pin 2 to relay COM, RELAY pin 1 to screen +, RELAY pin 2 stays GND but wires to screen −).
Mounting: sit this board near the Mean Well PSU so the PSU IN jumper stays short (~5–10 cm). M3 nylon standoffs from the same kit used for the main board.
PSU V+ / V− split point (read once, applies to both 12 V power board AND motor fuse board): the Mean Well LRS-150-12 has one V+ screw terminal and one V− screw terminal, but two boards need 12 V. Pick one:
  • Pigtail method (preferred): twist two ~18 AWG wire tails together at the V+ screw and clamp both under the terminal; same at V−. One tail per board's PSU IN plug. Solid, low-impedance, no extra hardware.
  • Wago 221 lever-nut method: one 18 AWG from V+ to a 3-port Wago, then two 18 AWG out (one per board). Same at V−. Easier service, slightly more impedance.
Keep the motor fuse board's PSU IN run short (≤15 cm) — every cm of wire is series inductance between the bulk cap and the PSU, which is exactly what the cap is supposed to mask. The 12 V power board's PSU IN run can be whatever length is convenient (low-current path).
GND star topology — DO NOT bond the two boards' GND rails together. Each board's GND rail returns to Mean Well V− through its own dedicated PSU IN pin 1 wire. The star is formed at the V− terminal itself, not on either board. Adding a "safety" jumper between the 12 V power board's GND rail and the motor fuse board's GND rail creates a ground loop — actuator current then has two return paths, the high-current path develops a voltage offset against the logic-ground rail, and logic-level signals get noisy. If something looks like it needs a jumper, it doesn't.

Motor fuse board (separate small board)

Carries the high-current actuator path: PSU 12 V comes in, splits to two 5 A fast-blow fuses (one per BTS B+ feed) for per-actuator selectivity, then exits to the two BTS7960 modules via dedicated Phoenix plugs. The 1000 µF bulk cap and the P6KE15CA TVS live on this board (not on the 12 V power board) — both exist to handle BTS-side inrush + actuator back-EMF transients, and need to sit electrically close to the BTS B+ pads to do their job.

Why per-actuator fusing, not a shared 8 A: a short on one actuator's wiring only blows that actuator's fuse — the other side remains operable, and firmware's per-BTS R_IS/L_IS current-sense reads the dead side as "current dropped to ~0 → end of travel" and cuts PWM cleanly. The 5 A rating sits snugly above PA-04 running current (~2–3 A) and below stall (~5–7 A), so any sustained stall trips promptly. Soft-start (firmware ramps PWM 0→100 % over ~200 ms) keeps inrush from nuisance-tripping. The 5 A fast-blow comes from the same Sunxeke kit as the 3 A — no new parts buy.

How to read this: the +12 V rail along the bottom carries pre-fuse raw PSU 12 V — it's tapped by the bulk cap, the TVS, and the bottom of each fuse holder. Each fuse's top pad has a dedicated wire going up to its BTS plug pin 2 (post-fuse #1 → BTS #1, post-fuse #2 → BTS #2 — never shared). Solid red = +12 V on the front side; dashed black = GND on the back side. Where a solid lead crosses the dashed GND rail with no joint dot, the two are on different sides of the board — no electrical connection.

Polarity: the bulk cap is polarized — confirm the white "−" stripe on the can faces the GND lead before soldering. The P6KE15CA TVS is bidirectional (CA suffix = "Common Anode" symmetric clamp) so either lead can land on +12 V; orientation doesn't matter. Leave both 5 A fuse cartridges OUT of their holders until all soldering + multimeter checks are complete.

2× BTS7960 H-bridges (one per actuator)

Each PA-04 gets its own IBT-2 module — independent current sensing per actuator means each side can be cut independently when its endstop trips, and the firmware can confirm retract-end on each motor separately by watching its IS pin collapse to ~0 V. Both modules share the same control signals so they move in lockstep; the only thing per-module is the M+/M- output and the analog current-sense readings.

  • B+ / B− (both modules) → each module's pair pulls from its dedicated BTS OUT plug on the motor fuse board (Diagram #6) — BTS #1's B+/B− from that board's BTS #1 OUT plug, BTS #2's from BTS #2 OUT. Each plug carries one 5 A fast-blow fuse in series with B+; the 1000 µF bulk cap and the P6KE15CA TVS live on the motor fuse board adjacent to the rail, so they're electrically close to both BTS modules.
  • M+ / M− → one pair per module → that module's PA-04 actuator (red/black motor leads)
  • VCC / GND (both modules) → 5 V rail (LM2596 #1 OUT+) + GND rail. BTS7960 logic is rated for 5 V, and the same rail powers the ESP32's 5V/VIN pin, so it's a single supply net.
  • RPWM / LPWM (both modules) → tied together → two ESP32 PWM pins total (extend / retract). Use LEDC at 20–25 kHz to stay inaudible. Both modules see the same PWM, so they move in sync.
  • R_EN / L_EN (both modules) → split per-BTS, not ganged: BTS #1's R_EN + L_EN tie together and go to GPIO 27; BTS #2's R_EN + L_EN tie together and go to GPIO 19. Firmware drops one EN LOW on its actuator's endstop trip → that BTS goes hi-Z, only that actuator stops while the other keeps moving. Confirmed against §9 Diagram #1 GPIO map and the main board wire-list table.
  • R_IS / L_IS → 4 ESP32 analog inputs total (2 per module). Per-actuator current readings; firmware uses R_IS during CLOSING to detect retract end-of-travel (current drops to ~0 when each PA-04's internal limit opens its motor circuit). The high end of the IS range saturates the ADC at the on-module 10 kΩ pulldown — irrelevant here, since "current dropped to zero" is a low-end transition that the ADC reads cleanly.

PA-04 actuators (no external endstops)

Each actuator's red/black wires connect directly to one M+/M− pair on its own BTS7960 module. The PA-04's built-in limit switches handle end-of-travel safety internally — when the shaft hits either end, the internal switch opens the motor circuit. Current drops to near-zero; the ESP32 sees that via that module's R_IS/L_IS and cuts PWM. No external endstop wiring needed.

F-1001/5V dual relay (screen + fan control)

  • Channel 1 (screen trigger): ESP32 GPIO → small NPN/MOSFET buffer → relay coil → contacts pass 12V to the existing screen's 5–12V trigger wire
  • Channel 2: unused (free for future expansion)

IRLZ44N MOSFET fan PWM (on dedicated fan-driver perfboard)

Both AIRPLATE S3 fans run as DC fans off a dedicated 6.5 V rail produced by an external LM2596 buck (fed 12 V from the future 12 V board). A single logic-level NMOSFET on the low side chops the GND return at the PWM duty set by ESP32 GPIO 17, giving us soft-start, variable speed by use case (quiet during viewing, full blast during cooldown), and complete elimination of AC routing inside the projector box. The factory wall warts, inline speed controllers, and SSR-25DA all get cut out and discarded. The MOSFET + gate network + diode all live on the fan-driver perfboard (diagram earlier in this section). The main control board hands the fan-driver board only two wires via Plug 4 (FAN SIG): GND and GPIO 17 PWM. The fan-driver board's +6.5 V supply comes from its own BUCK IN plug, fed by that external buck.

  • LM2596 #2 dialed to 6.5 V before installation (turn the multi-turn trim pot while watching a multimeter on OUT+/OUT−). 12 V in from the future 12 V board's output, ~26 W headroom on the buck against ~3 W typical fan draw.
  • IRLZ44N NMOSFET in TO-220, low-side switch on the fan GND return path. Drain → FAN return (fan-driver OUTPUT plug pin 2 → both fans − leads). Source → fan-driver board GND rail. Gate → 220 Ω → fan-driver INPUT plug pin 3 (which carries GPIO 17 over from the main board). Tab is internally tied to drain — keep it isolated from anything grounded if the back plane is conductive.
  • 220 Ω gate resistor in series with the GPIO-17 input. Limits the gate-charge inrush current during PWM switching transitions so the ESP32 pin isn't fighting the MOSFET's gate capacitance across the inter-board cable.
  • 10 kΩ gate pulldown from the gate node to GND. Holds the MOSFET hard off when the ESP32 pin is floating (boot, reset, OTA in progress) so the fans don't run wild during the first ~200 ms after power-up.
  • RB085T-60 Schottky diode across the fan terminals as a flyback — this is the TO-220 3-pin common-cathode dual variant: pin 2 (middle, K, also the tab) to the +6.5 V net; pins 1 and 3 (both anodes, A1 and A2) tied together and to the FAN return / MOSFET drain net. Clamps the inductive spike when the MOSFET cuts current so the MOSFET's drain doesn't see a 30 V transient and avalanche. Paralleling both anodes gives ~16 A current headroom against ~1 A actual fan draw — wild overkill, but the jumper is free. Tab is at +6.5 V, not GND — keep it isolated from any grounded surface.
  • Fan splice: cut each AIRPLATE fan's USB cable a few inches past the fan body, discard everything downstream (controller, wall wart). Tin the two conductors. Solder both reds together onto the fan-driver OUTPUT plug pin 1, both blacks onto pin 2.
  • Firmware: dedicate LEDC channel 2 to GPIO 17, run at ~20 kHz (inaudible, same as actuator PWM). Map ESPHome output + fan components for 0–100 % speed control with a 200 ms soft-start ramp.

Wall button + projector IR repeater

  • Wall button: Square D X-Series SQR14106WH momentary rocker. One terminal to ESP32 GPIO 18 with internal pull-up enabled, other to logic GND (switch-to-ground pattern). Each press momentarily pulls GPIO 18 LOW; release floats it back HIGH via the pull-up. ESPHome's binary_sensor handles debouncing in software via delayed_on/delayed_off filters — no hardware RC needed. Firmware dispatches on the rising edge (on_press): both deployed-end endstops tripped ⇒ CLOSE, otherwise ⇒ OPEN. Release is not bound.
  • IR repeater: stays as-is, untouched. Projector power is independent of the lift control.
Firmware rule: never drive both PWM channels high simultaneously. To reverse direction: ramp the active side to 0, brief deadtime (~50 ms), then ramp the other side up. Run PWM at 20–25 kHz to stay inaudible. Each cycle ends with both actuators at their internal limits — sync drift auto-resets to zero every cycle, so the box can't accumulate tilt over time.
Soft-start is load-bearing for fuse sizing. Each actuator now sits behind its own 5 A fast-blow on the motor fuse board (Diagram #6) — snug above PA-04 running current (~2–3 A) and below stall (~5–7 A). That sizing assumes the firmware PWM ramp (0 → 100 % over ~200 ms) keeps inrush near steady-state. If you ever bench-test without the ramp — e.g., a quick PWM-100% pulse to verify direction — the inrush will spike to 12–14 A and instantly trip the 5 A. Always test with the ramp enabled. If the ramp gets removed from firmware later (refactor, reflash, OTA mistake), expect the per-actuator fuses to start tripping until it's restored. Selectivity: a short or jam on one actuator only blows that side's fuse; the other side keeps moving until firmware's R_IS reads "current dropped to ~0 → end of travel" on the dead side and cuts PWM cleanly.

Critical behaviors

  • Direction reversal deadtime: 50 ms minimum between dropping one PWM channel and raising the other — prevents BTS7960 shoot-through.
  • PWM frequency: 20–25 kHz via ESP32 LEDC. Above human hearing, no whine from the motor windings.
  • Soft-start / soft-stop ramps: ~500 ms accel and decel at each end of travel. Quieter motion, less mechanical shock.
  • Hardware watchdog: esp_task_wdt with 5 s timeout, fed from the main loop. Any firmware hang self-resets the MCU in seconds.
  • Power-outage behavior: on boot, default to CLOSED state with fans OFF. If the box was actually mid-cycle when power was lost, it's stuck in whatever position the actuators stopped at — user can press the button to recover (button press from CLOSED state ⇒ OPENING, which will run until limits hit and re-establish state). Fail-safe rather than fail-active.
  • Single-button dispatch driven by endstop state: a wall-button press checks the two deployed-end endstops — both tripped ⇒ CLOSE, otherwise ⇒ OPEN. This makes the wall switch a pure event source rather than a state mirror, so it never fights the remote, voice control, or HA-side commands. A press mid-CLOSING reverses cleanly to OPENING (open_lift stops close_lift first); a press during COOLDOWN cancels the timer and reopens; a press during FAULT is ignored.

Configuration that should be tunable

Expose these as ESPHome number entities (or simple compile-time constants) so they can be tweaked without re-flashing the firmware:

  • Cooldown duration (default 30 min) — adjust if 30 min feels too long or too short in practice
  • End-of-travel threshold (R_IS voltage below which retract is considered complete) — calibrate empirically with the real PA-04s on the bench
  • PWM ramp duration (default 500 ms) — slower = quieter, faster = snappier
  • Screen drop / retract delay relative to box motion (default ~3 sec post-close before retracting screen) — depends on how long the box takes to fully clear the screen path
  • Cruise PWM duty cycle (default 100%) — could be reduced for quieter travel at the cost of speed
What we explicitly didn't add: position feedback (hall-effect encoder on the actuators). The PA-04 has an optional feedback variant for ~$30 more per unit, but it's overkill here — we only need "open" and "closed" states, both of which the internal limits give us for free. Skipping feedback keeps the bill of materials simple and the firmware thinner.

  • Telnet client to AVR (TCP port 23) — sends mode command strings on wall-button state change; persistent settings mean we don't need to re-send on every Apple TV wake
  • PJLink client to projector (TCP port 4352) — power on during OPEN sequence, power off + cooldown wait during CLOSE
  • AVR monitor-state polling — for BOTH-mode auto-detection: when wall button is OPEN and AVR reports Out 1 active (Sony TV powered up), fire BOTH-mode commands; when TV powers off again, return to OUTDOOR-mode commands
  • Reconciliation on boot — re-send mode commands matching current wall-button position so AVR is correct after a power outage
  • Static IP / DHCP reservation for AVR + projector + ESP32 itself so the TCP endpoints don't move

"Alexa, turn on the projector" does exactly what pressing the wall button does: projector drops, AVR routes to outdoor mode, porch speakers come up, fan runs the open-and-cooldown cycle.
